What Is Food Carbon Footprint?

Food Carbon Footprint is a term to describe the environmental impact of food production. It is a numeric value measured in kg/tonnes of CO2 emitted into atmosphere

Let’s take a closer look at the environmental impacts of food production

how does food Production impact the environment?

Land Use

In food production, land use refers to the process of converting forests or grasslands into agricultural or grazing land. This activity generates carbon dioxide emissions which accounts for 24% of food emissions

animal feed and farm

Keeping livestock and farm translates to additional greenhouse gases from animal feed production and livestock emission (CO2 and methane from animal digestive system and manure). Direct livestock emissions contributes around 10% of Australia’s greenhouse gas emissions

PROCESSING AND TRANSPORT

Foods are often long distances away to reach customers. As a result, food transportation process can consume substantial amounts of fossil fuels. Transport emissions from transporting food account for around 10% of the total food carbon emission

PACKAGING

Food packaging is required to protect food from contamination and preserve food freshness. It also extends the shelf life of perishable food items, therefore reducing the possibility of food waste and its environmental impacts. However, excessive or non-reusable packaging might add an extra burden to the environment

Food Choices Myths

Flip The Cards and Debunk The Myths

Myth #1

Organic food has less carbon footprint because organic farming requires less fertilizer and pesticide compared to conventional farming

Fact #1

Organic farming requires more land compared to conventional farming to produce the same amount of yields, resulting in higher overall carbon footprint

Myth #2

Buying local absolves me of reducing my carbon footprint

Fact #2

Buying local slightly reduces carbon footprint, but switching to an alternative food item can bring more meaningful change

Myth #3

Fruit and Vegetables always generate less carbon footprint compared to Meat

Fact #3

Not always, out of season fruit and vegetables need to be transported from distant location resulting in higher environmental impact

Myth #4

Buying in bulk reduces food packaging which resulted in less food carbon footprint

Fact #4

Be cautious, buying in bulk increases the probability of food waste and some foods require refrigerator that contributes to additional carbon footprint
